Essential Mathematical Skills ▼

Key Math Concepts for Chemistry:

1. Scientific Notation & Significant Figures

  • Express very large/small numbers: 6.022 × 10²³
  • Multiplication: Add exponents (10³ × 10² = 10⁵)
  • Division: Subtract exponents (10⁵ ÷ 10² = 10³)
  • Significant figures in calculations: Use least precise measurement

2. Dimensional Analysis (Unit Conversions)

Example: Convert 2.5 kg to grams
2.5 kg × (1000 g/1 kg) = 2500 g

Complex Example: Convert 65 mph to m/s
65 mph × (1609 m/1 mile) × (1 hr/3600 s) = 29.1 m/s

3. Logarithms (for pH, equilibrium)

  • log(10ˣ) = x
  • 10^(log x) = x
  • pH = -log[H⁺]
  • [H⁺] = 10^(-pH)

Atomic Structure and Properties

Understanding atomic structure is fundamental to all chemistry. This section covers quantum mechanics, electron configuration, and periodic trends - concepts that underlie chemical bonding, reactions, and properties.
Historical Development & Modern Atomic Theory ▼

Key Scientists and Discoveries:

  • Dalton (1808): Atomic theory - atoms are indivisible particles
  • Thomson (1897): Discovered electron, "plum pudding" model
  • Rutherford (1909): Gold foil experiment, nuclear model
  • Bohr (1913): Quantized energy levels for electrons
  • de Broglie (1924): Wave nature of electrons
  • Heisenberg (1927): Uncertainty principle
  • Schrödinger (1926): Wave equation, modern quantum model
Modern Atomic Model:
Atoms consist of a dense nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in probability clouds called orbitals. Electron behavior is described by wave functions and quantum numbers.
Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment Conclusions:
• Most alpha particles passed through → atoms are mostly empty space
• Some particles deflected → positive nucleus exists
• Few particles bounced back → nucleus is small and dense
Quantum Mechanics & Atomic Orbitals ▼

Four Quantum Numbers:

n (Principal Quantum Number): 1, 2, 3, 4...
Determines energy level and size of orbital

ℓ (Angular Momentum): 0 to (n-1)
Determines shape: s(0), p(1), d(2), f(3)

mℓ (Magnetic): -ℓ to +ℓ
Determines orientation in space

ms (Spin): +½ or -½
Electron spin direction

Orbital Shapes and Capacities:

  1. s orbitals: Spherical, 1 orbital per sublevel, holds 2 electrons
  2. p orbitals: Dumbbell-shaped, 3 orbitals per sublevel, holds 6 electrons
  3. d orbitals: Complex shapes, 5 orbitals per sublevel, holds 10 electrons
  4. f orbitals: Very complex, 7 orbitals per sublevel, holds 14 electrons
Practice Problem:
Give the four quantum numbers for the last electron in sulfur (S).

Solution:
S: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴
Last electron is in 3p orbital
n = 3, ℓ = 1, mℓ = 0 (following Hund's rule), ms = +½
Electron Configuration & Orbital Filling ▼

Principles Governing Electron Configuration:

1. Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first
2. Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have identical quantum numbers
3. Hund's Rule: Single electrons occupy orbitals before pairing
Orbital Filling Order:
1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d¹⁰ 4p⁶ 5s² 4d¹⁰ 5p⁶ 6s² 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6p⁶ 7s² 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰

How to Write Electron Configurations:

  1. Find the element's atomic number (= number of electrons)
  2. Fill orbitals in order of increasing energy
  3. Use superscripts to show number of electrons in each orbital
  4. For ions: add electrons for negative ions, remove for positive ions
Examples:
Iron (Fe, Z=26): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶
Fe³⁺: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d⁵ (remove 4s² and one 3d)
Noble Gas Notation: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶
⚡ Exam Alert: Know exceptions! Cr: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵ and Cu: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰ (half-filled and filled d subshells are more stable)
Periodic Trends (Detailed Analysis) ▼

1. Atomic Radius

Definition: Half the distance between nuclei of two bonded atoms
Trend: Decreases left→right, increases top→bottom
Reason: Increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer; additional electron shells increase size

2. Ionization Energy

M(g) → M⁺(g) + e⁻ ΔH = IE₁
First IE: Energy to remove outermost electron
Trend: Increases left→right, decreases top→bottom
Ionization Energy Patterns:
• Large jumps occur when removing electrons from inner shells
• Na: 1st IE = 496 kJ/mol, 2nd IE = 4562 kJ/mol (huge jump!)
• This confirms Na forms Na⁺, not Na²⁺

3. Electron Affinity

M(g) + e⁻ → M⁻(g) ΔH = EA
Definition: Energy change when electron is added
Trend: Generally increases left→right (becomes more negative)

4. Electronegativity

Definition: Ability of atom to attract electrons in chemical bond
Scale: Pauling scale (0.7 to 4.0)
Most electronegative: F (4.0) > O (3.4) > N (3.0) > Cl (3.2)
Trend Practice:
Arrange in order of increasing atomic radius: F, Li, N, C

Answer: F < N < C < Li
(Period trend dominates over group trend for these elements)
Effective Nuclear Charge & Shielding ▼
Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff):
Zeff = Z - S
Where Z = atomic number, S = shielding electrons

Slater's Rules for Calculating Shielding:

  1. Electrons in same group: contribute 0.35 each (except 1s: 0.30)
  2. Electrons in (n-1) shell: contribute 0.85 each
  3. Electrons in (n-2) and lower: contribute 1.00 each
Example: Calculate Zeff for 3s electron in Na
Na: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹
• 2 electrons in 1s: 2 × 1.00 = 2.00
• 8 electrons in 2s²2p⁶: 8 × 0.85 = 6.80
• S = 2.00 + 6.80 = 8.80
• Zeff = 11 - 8.80 = 2.20
🧪 Lab Connection: Flame tests work because different elements have unique energy differences between orbitals, causing characteristic colors when electrons fall back to lower energy levels.

Chemical Bonding and Molecular Geometry

Chemical bonding determines how atoms combine to form compounds, affecting all chemical and physical properties. Master this topic to understand reactivity, molecular shapes, and intermolecular forces.
Types of Chemical Bonds ▼

1. Ionic Bonding

Formation: Metal transfers electrons to nonmetal
Electronegativity difference: > 1.7
Properties: High melting points, conduct when dissolved, brittle
Lattice Energy: Energy required to separate ionic solid into gaseous ions
U ∝ (q₁ × q₂)/r
Higher charges and smaller ions → higher lattice energy

2. Covalent Bonding

Formation: Sharing of electron pairs between nonmetals
Types:
• Nonpolar covalent: ΔEN = 0-0.4
• Polar covalent: ΔEN = 0.4-1.7
• Single, double, triple bonds possible

3. Metallic Bonding

Model: "Sea of electrons" - delocalized electrons move freely
Properties: Conductivity, malleability, ductility, metallic luster
Bond Type Practice:
Classify the bonding in: CaF₂, H₂O, Cu, CO₂

Answers:
• CaF₂: Ionic (ΔEN = 4.0-1.0 = 3.0)
• H₂O: Polar covalent (ΔEN = 3.4-2.1 = 1.3)
• Cu: Metallic
• CO₂: Polar covalent bonds, but nonpolar molecule
Lewis Structures (Complete Method) ▼

Step-by-Step Lewis Structure Method:

  1. Count total valence electrons
    • Add electrons for negative charges
    • Subtract electrons for positive charges
  2. Determine central atom
    • Usually least electronegative (except H)
    • Often the element that appears only once
  3. Draw single bonds to surrounding atoms
  4. Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs
  5. Check octet rule (except H wants 2, Be wants 4)
  6. Form multiple bonds if central atom lacks octet
  7. Check formal charges and minimize if needed
Formal Charge Formula:
FC = (valence e⁻) - (lone pair e⁻) - ½(bonding e⁻)

Best Lewis Structure Rules:
• Formal charges closest to zero
• Negative charges on most electronegative atoms
• Total formal charges = molecular charge
Complex Example: SO₄²⁻
1. Total electrons: 6 + 4(6) + 2 = 32 electrons
2. Central atom: S (less electronegative than O)
3. Draw S-O single bonds: uses 8 electrons
4. Complete octets: 24 more electrons needed
5. Check formal charges and add double bonds as needed
6. Best structure has S with expanded octet, minimal formal charges
⚡ Common Exam Molecules:
Practice these: CO₂, NH₃, H₂O, SO₂, ClO₃⁻, NO₃⁻, PCl₅, SF₆
Know exceptions: BF₃ (incomplete octet), PCl₅ (expanded octet)

Electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) repel and arrange to minimize repulsion

• Lone pairs take up more space than bonding pairs
• This causes smaller bond angles (NH₃: 107° vs. 109.5°)
• For expanded octets: lone pairs occupy equatorial positions first
• Double/triple bonds count as ONE electron pair region
Geometry Practice:
Predict geometry and bond angles for: CH₂O, ClF₃, XeF₄

Solutions:
• CH₂O: Trigonal planar (~120°) - double bond counts as one region
• ClF₃: T-shaped (~90°) - 5 regions, 2 lone pairs
• XeF₄: Square planar (90°) - 6 regions, 2 lone pairs
Polarity and Intermolecular Forces ▼

Molecular Polarity

Determining if a Molecule is Polar:

  1. Determine if individual bonds are polar (ΔEN > 0.4)
  2. Draw the molecular geometry
  3. Determine if polar bonds cancel out due to symmetry
  4. If they don't cancel → polar molecule
Polarity Examples:
• CO₂: Polar bonds, linear geometry → nonpolar (bonds cancel)
• H₂O: Polar bonds, bent geometry → polar (bonds don't cancel)
• CCl₄: Polar bonds, tetrahedral → nonpolar (symmetric)
• CHCl₃: Polar bonds, tetrahedral → polar (asymmetric)

Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Force TypeStrengthRequirementsExamples
London DispersionWeakestAll moleculesCH₄, Br₂
Dipole-DipoleMediumPolar moleculesHCl, SO₂
Hydrogen BondingStrongestH-F, H-O, H-NH₂O, NH₃, HF
Ion-DipoleVery StrongIon + polar moleculeNa⁺ in water
London Dispersion Forces:
• Present in ALL molecules
• Caused by temporary dipoles
• Strength increases with molecular size/surface area
• Explains why I₂ is solid, F₂ is gas at room temperature
🧪 Lab Applications:
• Solubility: "Like dissolves like" - polar solvents dissolve polar solutes
• Boiling points: Stronger IMFs → higher boiling points
• Chromatography: Different IMFs cause compound separation

Hybridization Theory

Concept: Atomic orbitals combine to form hybrid orbitals that better explain molecular geometry and bonding

  1. Draw Lewis structure
  2. Count electron pair regions around central atom
  3. Match to hybridization scheme
  4. Remember: lone pairs occupy hybrid orbitals too

Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds

Sigma Bonds: Head-to-head overlap, can rotate, stronger
Pi Bonds: Side-to-side overlap, restrict rotation, weaker

• Single bond: 1σ
• Double bond: 1σ + 1π
• Triple bond: 1σ + 2π

Stoichiometry and Chemical Reactions

Stoichiometry is the quantitative relationship between reactants and products in chemical reactions. Master this for success in all calculation-based chemistry problems.
The Mole Concept (Advanced) ▼
Key Relationships:
1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number)
1 mole = atomic/molecular mass in grams
1 mole gas at STP = 22.4 L
Molarity = moles solute / L solution

Conversion Roadmap:

Mass (g) ↔ Moles ↔ Particles ↔ Volume (L, at STP)

Use dimensional analysis for ALL conversions!

Mole Conversion Strategy:

  1. Identify what you have and what you want
  2. Set up conversion factors (units must cancel)
  3. Calculate and check units
  4. Verify answer makes sense
Multi-Step Practice:
How many oxygen atoms are in 25.0 g of Ca(NO₃)₂?

Solution:
1. Molar mass Ca(NO₃)₂ = 40.1 + 2(14.0 + 3×16.0) = 164.1 g/mol
2. 25.0 g × (1 mol/164.1 g) × (6 O atoms/1 formula unit) × (6.022×10²³ atoms/mol)
3. = 5.50×10²³ oxygen atoms

Balancing Chemical Equations (Complete Method) ▼

Systematic Balancing Method:

  1. Write skeleton equation with correct formulas
  2. Count atoms of each element on both sides
  3. Balance one element at a time
    • Start with most complex molecule
    • Balance metals first, then nonmetals
    • Save H and O for last
  4. Use whole number coefficients
  5. Verify all atoms balance
Complex Example: Balance Al₂(SO₄)₃ + Ca(OH)₂ → Al(OH)₃ + CaSO₄

Step-by-step:
1. Al: 2 on left, 1 on right → need coefficient 2 for Al(OH)₃
2. S: 3 on left, 1 on right → need coefficient 3 for CaSO₄
3. Ca: 1 on left, 3 on right → need coefficient 3 for Ca(OH)₂
4. Check all atoms balance

Balanced: Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 3Ca(OH)₂ → 2Al(OH)₃ + 3CaSO₄

Special Cases:

  • Combustion: CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (balance C, H, then O)
  • Redox reactions: May need half-reaction method
  • Fractional coefficients: Multiply all by common denominator
⚡ Exam Strategy: Always double-check by counting atoms. A common mistake is forgetting to multiply coefficients by subscripts!
Limiting Reactants & Percent Yield (Complete) ▼
Limiting Reactant: The reactant that is completely consumed first, limiting product formation
Excess Reactant: Reactant(s) left over after reaction stops

Limiting Reactant Problem Strategy:

  1. Balance the equation
  2. Convert given masses to moles
  3. Calculate theoretical product from each reactant
  4. Identify limiting reactant (gives least product)
  5. Calculate theoretical yield using limiting reactant
  6. Calculate percent yield if actual yield given
Complete Example:
2Al + 3Cl₂ → 2AlCl₃
Given: 5.40 g Al and 9.45 g Cl₂. Actual yield: 10.2 g AlCl₃

Solution:
1. Moles Al: 5.40 g ÷ 26.98 g/mol = 0.200 mol
2. Moles Cl₂: 9.45 g ÷ 70.90 g/mol = 0.133 mol
3. Product from Al: 0.200 mol Al × (2 mol AlCl₃/2 mol Al) = 0.200 mol AlCl₃
4. Product from Cl₂: 0.133 mol Cl₂ × (2 mol AlCl₃/3 mol Cl₂) = 0.0887 mol AlCl₃
5. Cl₂ is limiting (gives less product)
6. Theoretical yield: 0.0887 mol × 133.34 g/mol = 11.8 g
7. Percent yield: (10.2 g/11.8 g) × 100% = 86.4%
Key Formulas:
Percent Yield = (Actual Yield/Theoretical Yield) × 100%
Atom Economy = (MW desired product/MW all products) × 100%
💡 Common Mistakes:
• Using wrong mole ratio from balanced equation
• Forgetting to identify limiting reactant
• Using excess reactant for final calculation
• Unit errors in conversions
