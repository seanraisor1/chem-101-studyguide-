Essential Mathematical Skills ▼

Key Math Concepts for Chemistry:

1. Scientific Notation & Significant Figures

  • Express very large/small numbers: 6.022 × 10²³
  • Multiplication: Add exponents (10³ × 10² = 10⁵)
  • Division: Subtract exponents (10⁵ ÷ 10² = 10³)
  • Significant figures in calculations: Use least precise measurement

2. Dimensional Analysis (Unit Conversions)

Example: Convert 2.5 kg to grams
2.5 kg × (1000 g/1 kg) = 2500 g

Complex Example: Convert 65 mph to m/s
65 mph × (1609 m/1 mile) × (1 hr/3600 s) = 29.1 m/s

3. Logarithms (for pH, equilibrium)

  • log(10ˣ) = x
  • 10^(log x) = x
  • pH = -log[H⁺]
  • [H⁺] = 10^(-pH)

Atomic Structure and Properties

Understanding atomic structure is fundamental to all chemistry. This section covers quantum mechanics, electron configuration, and periodic trends - concepts that underlie chemical bonding, reactions, and properties.
Historical Development & Modern Atomic Theory ▼

Key Scientists and Discoveries:

  • Dalton (1808): Atomic theory - atoms are indivisible particles
  • Thomson (1897): Discovered electron, "plum pudding" model
  • Rutherford (1909): Gold foil experiment, nuclear model
  • Bohr (1913): Quantized energy levels for electrons
  • de Broglie (1924): Wave nature of electrons
  • Heisenberg (1927): Uncertainty principle
  • Schrödinger (1926): Wave equation, modern quantum model
Modern Atomic Model:
Atoms consist of a dense nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in probability clouds called orbitals. Electron behavior is described by wave functions and quantum numbers.
Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment Conclusions:
• Most alpha particles passed through → atoms are mostly empty space
• Some particles deflected → positive nucleus exists
• Few particles bounced back → nucleus is small and dense
Quantum Mechanics & Atomic Orbitals ▼

Four Quantum Numbers:

n (Principal Quantum Number): 1, 2, 3, 4...
Determines energy level and size of orbital

ℓ (Angular Momentum): 0 to (n-1)
Determines shape: s(0), p(1), d(2), f(3)

mℓ (Magnetic): -ℓ to +ℓ
Determines orientation in space

ms (Spin): +½ or -½
Electron spin direction

Orbital Shapes and Capacities:

  1. s orbitals: Spherical, 1 orbital per sublevel, holds 2 electrons
  2. p orbitals: Dumbbell-shaped, 3 orbitals per sublevel, holds 6 electrons
  3. d orbitals: Complex shapes, 5 orbitals per sublevel, holds 10 electrons
  4. f orbitals: Very complex, 7 orbitals per sublevel, holds 14 electrons
Practice Problem:
Give the four quantum numbers for the last electron in sulfur (S).

Solution:
S: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴
Last electron is in 3p orbital
n = 3, ℓ = 1, mℓ = 0 (following Hund's rule), ms = +½
Electron Configuration & Orbital Filling ▼

Principles Governing Electron Configuration:

1. Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first
2. Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have identical quantum numbers
3. Hund's Rule: Single electrons occupy orbitals before pairing
Orbital Filling Order:
1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d¹⁰ 4p⁶ 5s² 4d¹⁰ 5p⁶ 6s² 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6p⁶ 7s² 5f¹⁴ 6d¹⁰

How to Write Electron Configurations:

  1. Find the element's atomic number (= number of electrons)
  2. Fill orbitals in order of increasing energy
  3. Use superscripts to show number of electrons in each orbital
  4. For ions: add electrons for negative ions, remove for positive ions
Examples:
Iron (Fe, Z=26): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶
Fe³⁺: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d⁵ (remove 4s² and one 3d)
Noble Gas Notation: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶
⚡ Exam Alert: Know exceptions! Cr: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵ and Cu: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰ (half-filled and filled d subshells are more stable)
Periodic Trends (Detailed Analysis) ▼

1. Atomic Radius

Definition: Half the distance between nuclei of two bonded atoms
Trend: Decreases left→right, increases top→bottom
Reason: Increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer; additional electron shells increase size

2. Ionization Energy

M(g) → M⁺(g) + e⁻ ΔH = IE₁
First IE: Energy to remove outermost electron
Trend: Increases left→right, decreases top→bottom
Ionization Energy Patterns:
• Large jumps occur when removing electrons from inner shells
• Na: 1st IE = 496 kJ/mol, 2nd IE = 4562 kJ/mol (huge jump!)
• This confirms Na forms Na⁺, not Na²⁺

3. Electron Affinity

M(g) + e⁻ → M⁻(g) ΔH = EA
Definition: Energy change when electron is added
Trend: Generally increases left→right (becomes more negative)

4. Electronegativity

Definition: Ability of atom to attract electrons in chemical bond
Scale: Pauling scale (0.7 to 4.0)
Most electronegative: F (4.0) > O (3.4) > N (3.0) > Cl (3.2)
Trend Practice:
Arrange in order of increasing atomic radius: F, Li, N, C

Answer: F < N < C < Li
(Period trend dominates over group trend for these elements)
Effective Nuclear Charge & Shielding ▼
Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff):
Zeff = Z - S
Where Z = atomic number, S = shielding electrons

Slater's Rules for Calculating Shielding:

  1. Electrons in same group: contribute 0.35 each (except 1s: 0.30)
  2. Electrons in (n-1) shell: contribute 0.85 each
  3. Electrons in (n-2) and lower: contribute 1.00 each
Example: Calculate Zeff for 3s electron in Na
Na: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹
• 2 electrons in 1s: 2 × 1.00 = 2.00
• 8 electrons in 2s²2p⁶: 8 × 0.85 = 6.80
• S = 2.00 + 6.80 = 8.80
• Zeff = 11 - 8.80 = 2.20
🧪 Lab Connection: Flame tests work because different elements have unique energy differences between orbitals, causing characteristic colors when electrons fall back to lower energy levels.

Chemical Bonding and Molecular Geometry

Chemical bonding determines how atoms combine to form compounds, affecting all chemical and physical properties. Master this topic to understand reactivity, molecular shapes, and intermolecular forces.
Types of Chemical Bonds ▼

1. Ionic Bonding

Formation: Metal transfers electrons to nonmetal
Electronegativity difference: > 1.7
Properties: High melting points, conduct when dissolved, brittle
Lattice Energy: Energy required to separate ionic solid into gaseous ions
U ∝ (q₁ × q₂)/r
Higher charges and smaller ions → higher lattice energy

2. Covalent Bonding

Formation: Sharing of electron pairs between nonmetals
Types:
• Nonpolar covalent: ΔEN = 0-0.4
• Polar covalent: ΔEN = 0.4-1.7
• Single, double, triple bonds possible

3. Metallic Bonding

Model: "Sea of electrons" - delocalized electrons move freely
Properties: Conductivity, malleability, ductility, metallic luster
Bond Type Practice:
Classify the bonding in: CaF₂, H₂O, Cu, CO₂

Answers:
• CaF₂: Ionic (ΔEN = 4.0-1.0 = 3.0)
• H₂O: Polar covalent (ΔEN = 3.4-2.1 = 1.3)
• Cu: Metallic
• CO₂: Polar covalent bonds, but nonpolar molecule
Lewis Structures (Complete Method) ▼

Step-by-Step Lewis Structure Method:

  1. Count total valence electrons
    • Add electrons for negative charges
    • Subtract electrons for positive charges
  2. Determine central atom
    • Usually least electronegative (except H)
    • Often the element that appears only once
  3. Draw single bonds to surrounding atoms
  4. Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs
  5. Check octet rule (except H wants 2, Be wants 4)
  6. Form multiple bonds if central atom lacks octet
  7. Check formal charges and minimize if needed
Formal Charge Formula:
FC = (valence e⁻) - (lone pair e⁻) - ½(bonding e⁻)

Best Lewis Structure Rules:
• Formal charges closest to zero
• Negative charges on most electronegative atoms
• Total formal charges = molecular charge
Complex Example: SO₄²⁻
1. Total electrons: 6 + 4(6) + 2 = 32 electrons
2. Central atom: S (less electronegative than O)
3. Draw S-O single bonds: uses 8 electrons
4. Complete octets: 24 more electrons needed
5. Check formal charges and add double bonds as needed
6. Best structure has S with expanded octet, minimal formal charges
⚡ Common Exam Molecules:
Practice these: CO₂, NH₃, H₂O, SO₂, ClO₃⁻, NO₃⁻, PCl₅, SF₆
Know exceptions: BF₃ (incomplete octet), PCl₅ (expanded octet)

VSEPR Theory: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion
Electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) repel and arrange to minimize repulsion

Predict geometry and bond angles for: CH₂O, ClF₃, XeF₄

• ClF₃: T-shaped (~90°) - 5 regions, 2 lone pairs
• XeF₄: Square planar (90°) - 6 regions, 2 lone pairs
Polarity and Intermolecular Forces ▼

Molecular Polarity

Determining if a Molecule is Polar:

  1. Determine if individual bonds are polar (ΔEN > 0.4)
  2. Draw the molecular geometry
  3. Determine if polar bonds cancel out due to symmetry
  4. If they don't cancel → polar molecule
Polarity Examples:
• CO₂: Polar bonds, linear geometry → nonpolar (bonds cancel)
• H₂O: Polar bonds, bent geometry → polar (bonds don't cancel)
• CCl₄: Polar bonds, tetrahedral → nonpolar (symmetric)
• CHCl₃: Polar bonds, tetrahedral → polar (asymmetric)

Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Force TypeStrengthRequirementsExamples
London DispersionWeakestAll moleculesCH₄, Br₂
Dipole-DipoleMediumPolar moleculesHCl, SO₂
Hydrogen BondingStrongestH-F, H-O, H-NH₂O, NH₃, HF
Ion-DipoleVery StrongIon + polar moleculeNa⁺ in water
London Dispersion Forces:
• Present in ALL molecules
• Caused by temporary dipoles
• Strength increases with molecular size/surface area
• Explains why I₂ is solid, F₂ is gas at room temperature
🧪 Lab Applications:
• Solubility: "Like dissolves like" - polar solvents dissolve polar solutes
• Boiling points: Stronger IMFs → higher boiling points
• Chromatography: Different IMFs cause compound separation

Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds

Sigma Bonds: Head-to-head overlap, can rotate, stronger
Pi Bonds: Side-to-side overlap, restrict rotation, weaker

• Single bond: 1σ
• Double bond: 1σ + 1π
• Triple bond: 1σ + 2π

Gases and Kinetic Molecular Theory

Gas behavior follows predictable mathematical relationships. Master gas laws for success in pressure, volume, temperature, and mole calculations that frequently appear on exams.
Gas Laws (Complete) ▼
Individual Gas Laws:
Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant T, n)
Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant P, n)
Gay-Lussac's Law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (constant V, n)
Avogadro's Law: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂ (constant P, T)

Combined Gas Law: (P₁V₁)/T₁ = (P₂V₂)/T₂
Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT
R ValueUnitsWhen to Use
0.0821L·atm/(mol·K)Pressure in atm, volume in L
8.314J/(mol·K)Energy calculations
62.4L·torr/(mol·K)Pressure in torr
Complete Gas Law Problem:
A 2.5 L balloon at 25°C and 1.2 atm is heated to 75°C. If the pressure increases to 1.8 atm, what is the new volume?

Solution:
Using combined gas law: (P₁V₁)/T₁ = (P₂V₂)/T₂
T₁ = 25°C + 273 = 298 K, T₂ = 75°C + 273 = 348 K
(1.2 atm × 2.5 L)/298 K = (1.8 atm × V₂)/348 K
V₂ = (1.2 × 2.5 × 348)/(298 × 1.8) = 1.94 L
Kinetic Molecular Theory ▼

Five Key Assumptions:

  1. Gas particles have negligible volume compared to container
  2. No intermolecular forces between gas particles
  3. Particles in constant, random motion - straight lines until collision
  4. Collisions are perfectly elastic - no energy lost
  5. Average kinetic energy ∝ absolute temperature
Key KMT Equations:
Average KE = (3/2)RT
Root Mean Square Speed: v_rms = √(3RT/M)
Graham's Law: Rate₁/Rate₂ = √(M₂/M₁)
⚡ Exam Applications:
• Lighter gases move faster at same temperature
• Real gases deviate from ideal at high P, low T
• Use KMT to explain gas law relationships

Solutions and Colligative Properties

Solution chemistry governs how substances dissolve, concentrate, and affect physical properties. Critical for understanding biological systems, industrial processes, and analytical chemistry.
Concentration Units (All Types) ▼
Major Concentration Units:
Molarity (M): mol solute / L solution
Molality (m): mol solute / kg solvent
Mass %: (g solute / g solution) × 100%
Mole Fraction (χ): mol component / total mol
Parts per million (ppm): (g solute / g solution) × 10⁶

Dilution Problems:

  1. Use M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ for dilutions
  2. Remember: moles of solute remain constant
  3. Final volume = initial volume + added solvent
Multi-Unit Problem:
Calculate molarity, molality, and mole fraction of 20.0 g NaCl in 200.0 g H₂O (final volume = 208 mL)

Solution:
Moles NaCl = 20.0 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.342 mol
• Molarity = 0.342 mol ÷ 0.208 L = 1.64 M
• Molality = 0.342 mol ÷ 0.200 kg = 1.71 m
• Moles H₂O = 200.0 g ÷ 18.02 g/mol = 11.10 mol
• χ_NaCl = 0.342 / (0.342 + 11.10) = 0.0299
Colligative Properties (Complete) ▼
Colligative Properties: Depend only on NUMBER of particles, not identity
Critical factor: van't Hoff factor (i) = particles per formula unit
Four Colligative Properties:
1. Vapor Pressure Lowering: ΔP = χ_solute × P°_solvent
2. Boiling Point Elevation: ΔT_b = K_b × m × i
3. Freezing Point Depression: ΔT_f = K_f × m × i
4. Osmotic Pressure: π = MRTi
SolventK_f (°C/m)K_b (°C/m)Normal fp/bp
Water1.860.520°C / 100°C
Benzene5.122.535.5°C / 80.1°C
💡 van't Hoff Factors:
• Nonelectrolytes (glucose, sucrose): i = 1
• NaCl, HCl: i ≈ 2
• CaCl₂, Na₂SO₄: i ≈ 3
• Al₂(SO₄)₃: i ≈ 5

Thermochemistry and Energetics

Energy changes drive all chemical reactions. Understanding enthalpy, entropy, and free energy is essential for predicting reaction spontaneity and calculating heat transfer.
Enthalpy and Calorimetry ▼
Fundamental Equations:
Heat Transfer: q = mcΔT
Enthalpy Change: ΔH = H_products - H_reactants
Calorimetry: q_system + q_surroundings = 0
Heat Capacity: q = CΔT
Sign Conventions:
• Endothermic: ΔH > 0 (system absorbs heat)
• Exothermic: ΔH < 0 (system releases heat)
• Heat flows from hot → cold
Calorimetry Problem:
25.0 g of hot metal (c = 0.45 J/g°C) at 95.0°C is dropped into 100.0 g water at 22.0°C. Final temperature is 24.3°C. What's the specific heat of water?

Solution:
q_metal = 25.0 g × 0.45 J/g°C × (24.3 - 95.0)°C = -798 J
q_water = -q_metal = +798 J
798 J = 100.0 g × c × (24.3 - 22.0)°C
c = 798 J ÷ (100.0 g × 2.3°C) = 3.47 J/g°C ≈ 4.18 J/g°C
Hess's Law and Formation Reactions ▼
Hess's Law: Total enthalpy change is independent of pathway
Standard Formation Reaction: 1 mole compound from elements in standard states
Hess's Law Applications:
ΔH°_rxn = Σ ΔH°_f(products) - Σ ΔH°_f(reactants)
ΔH°_rxn = Σ ΔH°_c(reactants) - Σ ΔH°_c(products)
ΔH°_rxn = Σ Bond energies broken - Σ Bond energies formed

Hess's Law Problem Strategy:

  1. Write target equation
  2. Manipulate given equations (reverse, multiply)
  3. Adjust ΔH values accordingly
  4. Add equations to get target equation
  5. Add corresponding ΔH values

Acids, Bases, and pH

Acid-base chemistry is fundamental to biological systems, industrial processes, and analytical chemistry. Master pH calculations, buffer systems, and titration curves for exam success.
pH and pOH Calculations (Complete) ▼
Fundamental Relationships:
pH = -log[H⁺]
pOH = -log[OH⁻]
pH + pOH = 14.00 (at 25°C)
K_w = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴
[H⁺] = 10^(-pH)
[OH⁻] = 10^(-pOH)
pH Scale:
• pH < 7: Acidic ([H⁺] > [OH⁻])
• pH = 7: Neutral ([H⁺] = [OH⁻])
• pH > 7: Basic ([H⁺] < [OH⁻])
• Each pH unit = 10× change in [H⁺]
Multi-Step pH Problem:
Calculate pH of 0.025 M Ba(OH)₂ solution

Solution:
Ba(OH)₂ → Ba²⁺ + 2OH⁻
[OH⁻] = 2 × 0.025 M = 0.050 M
pOH = -log(0.050) = 1.30
pH = 14.00 - 1.30 = 12.70
Weak Acids and Bases (ICE Tables) ▼
Equilibrium Expressions:
Weak Acid: K_a = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]
Weak Base: K_b = [BH⁺][OH⁻]/[B]
Relationship: K_a × K_b = K_w = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴

ICE Table Method:

  1. Initial: Write initial concentrations
  2. Change: Show changes (usually -x, +x)
  3. Equilibrium: Initial ± Change
  4. Substitute into K_a or K_b expression
  5. Solve for x (use approximations when valid)
ICE Table Example: 0.10 M CH₃COOH (K_a = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵)
CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻
I: 0.10 M, 0, 0
C: -x, +x, +x
E: 0.10-x, x, x
K_a = x²/(0.10-x) = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵
Approximation: x² ≈ 1.8 × 10⁻⁶
x = 1.3 × 10⁻³ M = [H⁺], pH = 2.87
Buffer Systems ▼
Buffer: Resists pH changes when small amounts of acid/base added
Components: Weak acid + conjugate base OR weak base + conjugate acid
Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation:
pH = pK_a + log([A⁻]/[HA])
pOH = pK_b + log([BH⁺]/[B])
⚡ Buffer Exam Tips:
• Best buffers: pK_a ≈ desired pH
• Buffer capacity highest when [acid] = [base]
• Common biological buffers: phosphate (pH 7.4)
• Adding acid consumes base; adding base consumes acid

Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium governs reaction completion and product distribution. Understanding equilibrium constants and Le Châtelier's principle is crucial for predicting reaction behavior.
Equilibrium Constants and Calculations ▼
For: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
K_c = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b (concentrations)
K_p = (P_C)^c(P_D)^d / (P_A)^a(P_B)^b (pressures)
Relationship: K_p = K_c(RT)^Δn
where Δn = (moles gaseous products) - (moles gaseous reactants)
Interpreting K Values:
• K >> 1 (K > 1000): Products strongly favored
• K << 1 (K < 0.001): Reactants strongly favored
• K ≈ 1 (0.001 < K < 1000): Significant amounts of both
Equilibrium Calculation:
For N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g), K_c = 0.36 at 100°C
If [N₂O₄] = 0.042 M at equilibrium, find [NO₂]

Solution:
K_c = [NO₂]² / [N₂O₄] = 0.36
0.36 = [NO₂]² / 0.042
[NO₂]² = 0.36 × 0.042 = 0.0151
[NO₂] = 0.123 M
Le Châtelier's Principle (Complete) ▼
Le Châtelier's Principle: When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to counteract the disturbance and reestablish equilibrium.
Stress AppliedEquilibrium ShiftExample
Add reactantRight (toward products)Add more N₂ → more NH₃
Remove productRight (toward products)Remove NH₃ → shift right
Increase temperatureToward endothermic sideHeat + reactants ⇌ products
Increase pressureToward fewer gas molecules4 mol gas ⇌ 2 mol gas
Add catalystNo shift (reaches equilibrium faster)K unchanged
Classic Example: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) + heat
• Add N₂ → shift right (more NH₃)
• Increase T → shift left (endothermic direction)
• Increase P → shift right (4 mol → 2 mol)
• Remove NH₃ → shift right (replace NH₃)

Stoichiometry and Chemical Reactions

Stoichiometry is the quantitative relationship between reactants and products in chemical reactions. Master this for success in all calculation-based chemistry problems.
The Mole Concept (Advanced) ▼
Key Relationships:
1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number)
1 mole = atomic/molecular mass in grams
1 mole gas at STP = 22.4 L
Molarity = moles solute / L solution

Conversion Roadmap:

Mass (g) ↔ Moles ↔ Particles ↔ Volume (L, at STP)

Use dimensional analysis for ALL conversions!

Mole Conversion Strategy:

  1. Identify what you have and what you want
  2. Set up conversion factors (units must cancel)
  3. Calculate and check units
  4. Verify answer makes sense
Multi-Step Practice:
How many oxygen atoms are in 25.0 g of Ca(NO₃)₂?

Solution:
1. Molar mass Ca(NO₃)₂ = 40.1 + 2(14.0 + 3×16.0) = 164.1 g/mol
2. 25.0 g × (1 mol/164.1 g) × (6 O atoms/1 formula unit) × (6.022×10²³ atoms/mol)
3. = 5.50×10²³ oxygen atoms

Balancing Chemical Equations (Complete Method) ▼

Systematic Balancing Method:

  1. Write skeleton equation with correct formulas
  2. Count atoms of each element on both sides
  3. Balance one element at a time
    • Start with most complex molecule
    • Balance metals first, then nonmetals
    • Save H and O for last
  4. Use whole number coefficients
  5. Verify all atoms balance
Complex Example: Balance Al₂(SO₄)₃ + Ca(OH)₂ → Al(OH)₃ + CaSO₄

Step-by-step:
1. Al: 2 on left, 1 on right → need coefficient 2 for Al(OH)₃
2. S: 3 on left, 1 on right → need coefficient 3 for CaSO₄
3. Ca: 1 on left, 3 on right → need coefficient 3 for Ca(OH)₂
4. Check all atoms balance

Balanced: Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 3Ca(OH)₂ → 2Al(OH)₃ + 3CaSO₄

Special Cases:

  • Combustion: CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (balance C, H, then O)
  • Redox reactions: May need half-reaction method
  • Fractional coefficients: Multiply all by common denominator
⚡ Exam Strategy: Always double-check by counting atoms. A common mistake is forgetting to multiply coefficients by subscripts!
Limiting Reactants & Percent Yield (Complete) ▼
Limiting Reactant: The reactant that is completely consumed first, limiting product formation
Excess Reactant: Reactant(s) left over after reaction stops

Limiting Reactant Problem Strategy:

  1. Balance the equation
  2. Convert given masses to moles
  3. Calculate theoretical product from each reactant
  4. Identify limiting reactant (gives least product)
  5. Calculate theoretical yield using limiting reactant
  6. Calculate percent yield if actual yield given
Complete Example:
2Al + 3Cl₂ → 2AlCl₃
Given: 5.40 g Al and 9.45 g Cl₂. Actual yield: 10.2 g AlCl₃

Solution:
1. Moles Al: 5.40 g ÷ 26.98 g/mol = 0.200 mol
2. Moles Cl₂: 9.45 g ÷ 70.90 g/mol = 0.133 mol
3. Product from Al: 0.200 mol Al × (2 mol AlCl₃/2 mol Al) = 0.200 mol AlCl₃
4. Product from Cl₂: 0.133 mol Cl₂ × (2 mol AlCl₃/3 mol Cl₂) = 0.0887 mol AlCl₃
5. Cl₂ is limiting (gives less product)
6. Theoretical yield: 0.0887 mol × 133.34 g/mol = 11.8 g
7. Percent yield: (10.2 g/11.8 g) × 100% = 86.4%
Key Formulas:
Percent Yield = (Actual Yield/Theoretical Yield) × 100%
Atom Economy = (MW desired product/MW all products) × 100%
💡 Common Mistakes:
• Using wrong mole ratio from balanced equation
• Forgetting to identify limiting reactant
• Using excess reactant for final calculation
• Unit errors in conversions

Reaction Kinetics

Kinetics studies reaction rates and mechanisms. Unlike thermodynamics (can it happen?), kinetics asks how fast will it happen? Essential for understanding catalysis and reaction pathways.
Reaction Rates and Rate Laws ▼
Rate Expressions:
For A → B: Rate = -Δ[A]/Δt = +Δ[B]/Δt
For aA + bB → cC: Rate = -(1/a)Δ[A]/Δt = -(1/b)Δ[B]/Δt = (1/c)Δ[C]/Δt

Rate Law: Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
where k = rate constant, m,n = orders (found experimentally)
Reaction Orders:
• Zero order: Rate independent of concentration
• First order: Rate ∝ [A]
• Second order: Rate ∝ [A]²
• Overall order = sum of individual orders

Finding Rate Law from Data:

  1. Compare experiments where only one concentration changes
  2. Rate₂/Rate₁ = ([A₂]/[A₁])^m
  3. Solve for order m
  4. Repeat for other reactants
  5. Calculate k using any experiment
Integrated Rate Laws ▼
Zero Order: [A] = [A]₀ - kt
Linear plot: [A] vs t, slope = -k

First Order: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ - kt
Linear plot: ln[A] vs t, slope = -k
Half-life: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k

Second Order: 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt
Linear plot: 1/[A] vs t, slope = k
⚡ Half-Life Key Points:
• First order: t₁/₂ constant (independent of [A]₀)
• Zero order: t₁/₂ = [A]₀/2k (depends on [A]₀)
• Second order: t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀) (depends on [A]₀)
Arrhenius Equation and Catalysis ▼
Arrhenius Equation:
k = Ae^(-Ea/RT)
ln k = ln A - Ea/RT
Two-temperature form:
ln(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/R)(1/T₁ - 1/T₂)
Energy Diagrams:
• Activation Energy (Ea): Minimum energy needed
• Catalyst: Lowers Ea, increases rate
• ΔH: Energy difference reactants → products
• Transition state: Highest energy point
🧪 Catalysis Applications:
• Enzymes: Biological catalysts (specific, efficient)
• Heterogeneous: Different phase (Pt surface)
• Homogeneous: Same phase (dissolved acid)
• Industrial: Haber process (Fe catalyst)

Redox Reactions and Electrochemistry

Oxidation-reduction reactions involve electron transfer and are fundamental to batteries, corrosion, and biological processes. Master oxidation states and balancing for exam success.
Oxidation States and Identification ▼

Rules for Assigning Oxidation States:

  1. Free elements: oxidation state = 0
  2. Monatomic ions: oxidation state = charge
  3. Hydrogen: usually +1 (except metal hydrides: -1)
  4. Oxygen: usually -2 (except peroxides: -1, F₂O: +2)
  5. Group 1 metals: always +1
  6. Group 2 metals: always +2
  7. Halogens: usually -1 (when most electronegative)
  8. Sum rule: sum = charge on species
Complex Examples:
Cr₂O₇²⁻: 2(Cr) + 7(-2) = -2 → Cr = +6
NH₄⁺: N + 4(+1) = +1 → N = -3
H₂O₂: 2(+1) + 2(O) = 0 → O = -1
CaH₂: Ca + 2(H) = 0 → H = -1
Redox Definitions:
• Oxidation: Loss of electrons, ↑ oxidation state
• Reduction: Gain of electrons, ↓ oxidation state
• Memory: OIL RIG (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain)
Balancing Redox Equations ▼

Half-Reaction Method (Acidic Solution):

  1. Separate into oxidation and reduction half-reactions
  2. Balance atoms other than H and O
  3. Balance O by adding H₂O
  4. Balance H by adding H⁺
  5. Balance charge by adding electrons
  6. Multiply half-reactions to equalize electrons
  7. Add half-reactions and cancel common terms
Complete Example: MnO₄⁻ + Fe²⁺ → Mn²⁺ + Fe³⁺ (acidic)

Reduction: MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O
Oxidation: Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + e⁻
Balance e⁻: Multiply oxidation by 5
Final: MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O
⚡ Basic Solution Modification:
After balancing in acidic conditions:
• Add OH⁻ to both sides to neutralize H⁺
• H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O
• Cancel excess water molecules
Electrochemical Cells ▼
Galvanic Cell: Spontaneous redox → electrical energy
Electrolytic Cell: Electrical energy → non-spontaneous redox
Cell Potential:
E°_cell = E°_cathode - E°_anode
Nernst Equation:
E = E° - (RT/nF)ln Q
E = E° - (0.0592/n)log Q (at 25°C)

Nuclear Chemistry

Nuclear chemistry involves changes in atomic nuclei, releasing enormous amounts of energy. Applications include medical imaging, carbon dating, nuclear power, and understanding stellar processes.

• Even-even nuclei most stable
Half-Life and Decay Kinetics ▼
First-Order Decay Kinetics:
N(t) = N₀e^(-λt)
ln(N(t)/N₀) = -λt
Half-Life: t₁/₂ = 0.693/λ
Activity: A = λN (decays per second)

Half-Life Problem Strategy:

  1. Identify what's given and what's asked
  2. Use N(t) = N₀(1/2)^(t/t₁/₂) for simple problems
  3. Use exponential form for complex calculations
  4. Remember: activity ∝ number of nuclei
Carbon-14 Dating Problem:
A fossil has 15% of the ¹⁴C activity of living tissue. How old is it? (t₁/₂ = 5730 years)

Solution:
N(t)/N₀ = 0.15
0.15 = e^(-λt) = (1/2)^(t/5730)
ln(0.15) = -(t/5730) × ln(2)
t = -5730 × ln(0.15)/ln(2) = 15,600 years
Nuclear Reactions and Energy ▼
Fission: Heavy nucleus splits → lighter nuclei + neutrons + energy
Fusion: Light nuclei combine → heavier nucleus + energy
Chain Reaction: Self-sustaining (critical mass needed)
Mass-Energy Equivalence:
E = mc²
Binding Energy: Energy to separate nucleus into nucleons
BE = (Δm)c² where Δm = mass defect
Famous Nuclear Reactions:
Fission: ²³⁵U + n → ⁹⁰Sr + ¹⁴³Xe + 3n + energy
Fusion (Sun): 4¹H → ⁴He + 2β⁺ + energy
Transmutation: ¹⁴N + ⁴He → ¹⁷O + ¹H
🧪 Applications:
• Medical: PET scans (¹⁸F), cancer treatment
• Energy: Nuclear power plants
• Dating: ¹⁴C (organic), U-Pb (rocks)
• Research: Particle accelerators, isotope production

Stoichiometry & Mole Problems ▼
Problem 1: How many grams of CO₂ are produced when 25.0 g of C₈H₁₈ undergoes complete combustion?

Solution:
1. Balance equation: 2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O
2. Molar mass C₈H₁₈ = 8(12.01) + 18(1.008) = 114.23 g/mol
3. Moles C₈H₁₈ = 25.0 g ÷ 114.23 g/mol = 0.219 mol
4. Moles CO₂ = 0.219 mol C₈H₁₈ × (16 mol CO₂/2 mol C₈H₁₈) = 1.75 mol
5. Mass CO₂ = 1.75 mol × 44.01 g/mol = 77.0 g CO₂
Problem 2: What is the limiting reactant when 15.0 g Al reacts with 25.0 g Cl₂?
2Al + 3Cl₂ → 2AlCl₃

Solution:
Moles Al = 15.0 g ÷ 26.98 g/mol = 0.556 mol
Moles Cl₂ = 25.0 g ÷ 70.90 g/mol = 0.353 mol
From Al: 0.556 mol Al × (2 mol AlCl₃/2 mol Al) = 0.556 mol AlCl₃
From Cl₂: 0.353 mol Cl₂ × (2 mol AlCl₃/3 mol Cl₂) = 0.235 mol AlCl₃
Cl₂ is limiting (produces less product)
Gas Law Problems ▼
Problem 3: A gas at 2.5 atm and 300 K occupies 5.0 L. What volume will it occupy at STP?

Solution:
Using combined gas law: (P₁V₁)/T₁ = (P₂V₂)/T₂
(2.5 atm × 5.0 L)/300 K = (1.0 atm × V₂)/273 K
V₂ = (2.5 × 5.0 × 273)/(300 × 1.0) = 11.4 L
Problem 4: How many moles of gas are in a 10.0 L container at 25°C and 2.0 atm?

Solution:
Using PV = nRT
n = PV/RT = (2.0 atm × 10.0 L)/(0.0821 L·atm/mol·K × 298 K)
n = 0.82 mol
Acid-Base and pH Problems ▼
Problem 5: Calculate the pH of 0.15 M NH₃ solution (Kb = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵)

Solution:
NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻
ICE table: I: 0.15, 0, 0; C: -x, +x, +x; E: 0.15-x, x, x
Kb = x²/(0.15-x) ≈ x²/0.15 = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵
x² = 2.7 × 10⁻⁶, x = 1.6 × 10⁻³ M = [OH⁻]
pOH = -log(1.6 × 10⁻³) = 2.80
pH = 14.00 - 2.80 = 11.20
Problem 6: What is the pH of a buffer containing 0.10 M CH₃COOH and 0.15 M CH₃COONa? (Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵)

Solution:
Using Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:
pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
pKa = -log(1.8 × 10⁻⁵) = 4.74
pH = 4.74 + log(0.15/0.10) = 4.74 + 0.18 = 4.92
Thermochemistry Problems ▼
Problem 7: Calculate ΔH for: C₂H₄(g) + H₂(g) → C₂H₆(g)
Given: ΔH°f[C₂H₄] = +52.3 kJ/mol, ΔH°f[C₂H₆] = -84.7 kJ/mol

Solution:
ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) - Σ ΔH°f(reactants)
ΔH°rxn = [-84.7] - [52.3 + 0] = -137.0 kJ/mol
Mixed Challenge Problems ▼
Challenge Problem 1: A 25.0 mL sample of 0.100 M HCl is titrated with 0.0500 M NaOH. Calculate the pH after 40.0 mL of NaOH has been added.

Solution:
Initial mol HCl = 0.0250 L × 0.100 M = 2.50 × 10⁻³ mol
Mol NaOH added = 0.0400 L × 0.0500 M = 2.00 × 10⁻³ mol
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (1:1 ratio)
Excess HCl = 2.50 × 10⁻³ - 2.00 × 10⁻³ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁴ mol
Total volume = 25.0 + 40.0 = 65.0 mL = 0.0650 L
[H⁺] = 5.0 × 10⁻⁴ mol ÷ 0.0650 L = 7.7 × 10⁻³ M
pH = -log(7.7 × 10⁻³) = 2.11

Basic Calculations & Units ▼
Mole Relationships:
n = m/M (moles = mass/molar mass)
N = n × NA (particles = moles × Avogadro's number)
V = n × 22.4 L (volume at STP = moles × 22.4 L/mol)

Concentration:
M = n/V (molarity = moles/liters)
m = n/kg solvent (molality)
χ = n₁/(n₁ + n₂ + ...) (mole fraction)
Dilution: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
Gas Laws ▼
Individual Gas Laws:
Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Gay-Lussac's Law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Combined & Ideal:
Combined: (P₁V₁)/T₁ = (P₂V₂)/T₂
Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT
Kinetic Energy: KE = (3/2)RT
RMS Speed: vrms = √(3RT/M)
Graham's Law: r₁/r₂ = √(M₂/M₁)
Thermochemistry ▼
Heat & Enthalpy:
q = mcΔT (heat = mass × specific heat × temp change)
q = nCΔT (heat = moles × molar heat capacity × temp change)
ΔH = qp (enthalpy change at constant pressure)
ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) - Σ ΔH°f(reactants)

Calorimetry:
qsystem + qsurroundings = 0
C = q/ΔT (heat capacity)
Acids, Bases & pH ▼
pH Relationships:
pH = -log[H⁺]
pOH = -log[OH⁻]
pH + pOH = 14.00
Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴

Weak Acids/Bases:
Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]
Kb = [BH⁺][OH⁻]/[B]
Ka × Kb = Kw
Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
Equilibrium & Kinetics ▼
Equilibrium:
Kc = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b
Kp = (PC)c(PD)d/(PA)a(PB)b
Kp = Kc(RT)Δn
Q vs K: Q < K (forward), Q = K (equilibrium), Q > K (reverse)

Kinetics:
Rate = k[A]m[B]n
First order: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ - kt, t₁/₂ = 0.693/k
Second order: 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt
Arrhenius: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT), ln(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/R)(1/T₁ - 1/T₂)
Colligative Properties & Nuclear ▼
Colligative Properties:
ΔTb = Kb × m × i (boiling point elevation)
ΔTf = Kf × m × i (freezing point depression)
π = MRTi (osmotic pressure)
ΔP = χsolute × P°solvent (vapor pressure lowering)

Nuclear:
N(t) = N₀e^(-λt) (radioactive decay)
t₁/₂ = 0.693/λ (half-life)
E = mc² (mass-energy equivalence)
A = λN (activity)
